Consonants and vowels pronunciation variations

We don't know exactly how Hebrew was pronounced originally but we do know that even in Biblical times there were different accents.

The pronunciation guide in alefbet is widely accepted, but as with any language, there is not really one correct pronunciation (although there are often strongly held opinions!).

The pronunciation in alefbet is mostly based on modern Israeli pronunciation of Hebrew. The main exception is the vowel which in modern Hebrew is usually silent but is pronounced with a short "uh" sound in some words in Biblical Hebrew.

Consonants

א and ע were originally gutteral sounds. Gutteral (literally 'of the throat') speech sounds are those with a primary place of articulation near the back of the oral cavity, where it is difficult to distinguish a sound's place of articulation and its phonation. In modern Hebrew, some speakers still pronounce ע as a gutteral consonant, others pronounce both these consonants as a glottal stopalefbet_consonants_and_vowels/Glottal_stop.ogg , similar to the sound in the middle of "uh-oh", and others do not pronounce these consonants at all.

The Wikipedia articles https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alef and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayin have more information about pronunciation of these consonants.

רalefbet_consonants_and_vowels/r According to the Wikipedia page https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resh there are several different ways of pronouncing this consonant:

  • In Modern Hebrew, the most common pronunciation is the voiced uvular fricative alefbet_consonants_and_vowels/Voiced_uvular_fricative.ogg
  • Sephardic and Mizrahi, and some Ashkenazi use a uvular trill alefbet_consonants_and_vowels/Uvular_trill.ogg or an alveolar trill alefbet_consonants_and_vowels/Alveolar_trill.ogg
  • These sounds are difficult for many native English-speakers, so ר is often pronounced by them as an alveolar approximant alefbet_consonants_and_vowels/Alveolar_approximant.ogg. (Note that this is the sound presented in alefbet.)

ת When this consonant has a dot in it, תּ, it is always pronounced "t" and is called "tav". Some Ashkenazy Jews pronounce ת without a dot as "s" and call this letter "sav". This may be closer to the original biblical pronunciation, which is believed to have been something like the "th" sound in bath or moth.

Why are ו and י both vowels and consonants? (A bit of history)

Biblical Hebrew was originally written only with consonants. This would be like writing in English "th grl pckd p hr pt". If you are an English speaker, you can probably figure out that this is "the girl picked up her " - but what did she pick up? "her pet"? or maybe "her pot"?

Similarly in Biblical Hebrew, using only consonants led to ambiguity, for example ים could be interpreted as "yom" (day) or "yam" (sea), so somewhere between the 9th or 8th century BCE , the consonants י and ו began to be used as vowel indicators (in addition to being consonants). In this case, ו was inserted giving יום to mark that this word was "yom". The consonant ה was used as a vowel indicator at the end of a word, for example, תורה ("tora", law, instruction, teaching), and the consonant א was also used occasionally to indicate a vowel. These vowel indicators are known as "matres lectionis" which is Latin for "mothers of reading".

This was somewhat helpful, but a reader still needed to be a Hebrew speaker to know that "yom" and "yam" were the only possibilities. In addition, the "matres lectionis" did not include all vowel sounds and the vowel marking was not systematic, so there were many words in the Tanakh where the the vowels were not marked.

Starting in biblical times, many Jews lived in places where the everyday language was not Hebrew, so over time there were fewer and fewer Jews who were able to speak or read Hebrew. As early as 200 BCE, it was necessary to translate the Tanakh into Greek for Jews living in Egypt (the first known translation, called the Septuagint). After the destruction of Jerusalem by the Romans in 70 CE, those who spoke Hebrew were scattered around the known world, joining the many Jews already elsewhere. The Hebrew language was spoken less by ordinary Jews, being kept alive primarily by Jewish scholars and religious leaders.

For nearly a thousand years, the texts of the Hebrew Bible were transmitted both in writing, as consonantal texts lacking much of the information on their pronunciation, and orally, as an accompanying reading tradition which supplied this information.

Around 500 CE, in an effort to preserve the vocalization of the Hebrew text, groups of Jewish scholars in Babylon and Jerusalem began to devise a system of vowel marks (also called "points"). These marks were inserted within and below the consonantal text. This system was refined and developed over the next 300-400 years, and became known as the Masoretic Text (the Masoretes is the name given to these groups of scholars). The Masoretes left the consonantal text unchanged and simply added the vowel signs to the existing letters, because by the time they were working the Hebrew Bible had become a fixed, sacred text.

Thus,

  • the spelling of "yom" became יוֹם with , the vowel marker for "o" added to י
  • the spelling of תורה, "torah", with the matres lectionis ה and ו became תוֹרָה.

In each case, the matres lectionis is now redundant in relation to pronunciation:

  • "yom" could be written יֹם (ie omitting the ו) and
  • "torah" could be written תֹרָ

In addition to the vowel pointings, the Masoretes added another set of marks, the cantillation marks. These marks preserved the tradition of liturgical chanting of the text by indicating the stressed syllable in each word, exactly how to punctuate the verses, and the melody to use for each word or phrase. The Masoretes also added textual commentary in the margins of the texts, giving important information about word forms and usage.

More about pronunciation of יהוה the Tetragrammaton

There is a longstanding Jewish prohibition on speaking God’s name aloud. The origin of this taboo — according to which, pronouncing God's name aloud is irreverent or possibly even a violation of the commandment not to take God’s name in vain — is not entirely clear. Some attribute it to a Temple practice in which only the High Priest was allowed to utter the name, and only when in the Temple and reciting the priestly blessing. Because of this prohibition, a variety of pseudonyms are used, such as Adonai (Lord), Elohim (God) and HaShem (The Name). Other possibilities, less gendered than Adonai, are El אֵלwords/w188_el or Yah יָהּalefbet/yah both of which are words used for God in the Tanakh, or Ruach רוּחַwords/w129_ruach meaning spirit, wind, breath.

When vowel marks were added to the biblical text in the first millenium, the Tetragrammaton was written with the vowels of "Adonai" to remind the reader to pronounce "Adonai" instead of the Tetragrammaton. A Christian scribe of the Middle Ages misunderstood this convention and transcribed the combination into Latin as "Jehovah" as if this were the name of God. This confused form entered English usage in several translations of the Bible.

It is thought that the original pronunciation of יהוה was "Yah-weh".
